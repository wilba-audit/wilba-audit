## Section 1: Brand Essence

Jess Morrell stands for one thing: **the belief that business owners built their businesses for freedom — and most of them accidentally built a prison instead.** She exists at the intersection of that pain and its solution, not as a technology salesperson, but as someone who has felt the same trap and found a practical, un-glamorous, immediately actionable way out of it. The brand occupies emotional territory that is equal parts frustration and possibility — the specific feeling a business owner gets at 6pm when they realise they missed three calls and the leads are gone, and the equally specific feeling three months later when their calendar books itself while they're at the beach. Jess's brand is the bridge between those two moments: clear-eyed about the problem, confident about the solution, and completely allergic to the kind of tech hype that makes business owners feel stupid for not already having it figured out. The brand is warm because it respects the people it serves, direct because their time is the most precious resource in their lives, and grounded because it operates from lived experience rather than theory.

## Section 2: The Positioning Statement

### The Statement

> For service business owners drowning in missed calls and manual admin, Jess Morrell is the AI automation guide who speaks plain English and delivers real dollar results — unlike the tech companies trying to sell them software or the business coaches who've never actually built what they're recommending — because she's running the same systems in her own business and can show you exactly what changed.

### Unpacked

**"For service business owners drowning in missed calls and manual admin"**
The audience is defined by a pain state, not a demographic. "Drowning" is the right word — it implies the water is already over their head and they didn't choose to be there. This is not about ambitious entrepreneurs who want to grow. It's about competent people who built something real and are now buried by it.

**"Jess Morrell is the AI automation guide"**
Not "expert." Not "consultant." Guide. A guide is someone who has been there before and will walk alongside you — not someone who stands at the front of a room and lectures. This is a crucial personality distinction that should show up in every piece of content.

**"who speaks plain English and delivers real dollar results"**
Two-part promise. Plain English addresses the fear (AI feels complex and inaccessible). Real dollar results addresses the priority (I don't care about technology, I care about revenue). Both must be present in the brand at all times — neither alone is enough.

**"unlike tech companies trying to sell them software or business coaches who've never built what they're recommending"**
The competitive differentiation is stated as a contrast, not an attack. It names the frustration that already exists in the audience — they've already encountered both of these and found them wanting. Jess is the third option they didn't know existed.

**"because she's running the same systems in her own business"**
The reason to believe. This is not "I studied this" or "I've worked with clients." It's "I live inside this." The AIOS, the audit pipeline, the content generation machine — all of it is running on Jess's own operation. That's the credibility that can't be faked or bought.

## Section 3: Competitive Landscape & Differentiation

### The Three Competitors

**Category 1: Software Companies and SaaS Brands**
*(GoHighLevel, Dialpad, Smith.ai, Aircall, Talkdesk, RingCentral)*

What they do in content: Product demos, feature listicles, "AI is the future" proclamations, B2B case studies with enterprise language, webinars that feel like sales calls.

Where they fail: They speak to a procurement manager, not a business owner who answers their own phone. Their content is designed to pass a shortlist evaluation, not create an emotional connection. They have no face. There is no person to trust — only a logo. For the service business owner who is already scared of technology, a faceless SaaS brand reinforces every fear: this will be complicated, impersonal, and when something goes wrong, I'll be on hold with a call centre.

Where Jess steps in: She is the human face of the outcome these tools produce. She doesn't compete with the software — she sits above it, as the trusted guide who selects the right tool, sets it up correctly, and guarantees it works for your specific business.

---

**Category 2: Tech Bros and AI Influencers**
*(The dominant personality in AI content — male, 25–35, often US-based, talks about "prompt engineering," "10x your productivity," "I made $47K in 30 days with AI")*

What they do in content: Hustle signalling, AI tool stacking, productivity hacks framed as secrets, "I quit my job with AI" thumbnails, content that treats AI as a status symbol rather than a practical tool.

Where they fail: Their audience is other young men who want to make money fast — not a 45-year-old physio in regional Victoria who just wants her afternoons back. They have almost no presence in the service business owner space, and when they show up, they're completely unrelatable. Their language is foreign, their examples are irrelevant, and their energy is exhausting. The physio, the plumber, the real estate agent — they scroll past this content in under a second.

Where Jess steps in: She speaks the language of a business owner, not a content creator. Her AI knowledge is applied, not theoretical. She doesn't talk about AI as a wealth hack — she talks about it as the thing that stopped her clients missing calls at 3pm on a Tuesday. That's a completely different emotional register, and it's the one that converts.

---

**Category 3: Generic Business Growth Coaches**
*(The "work smarter not harder" brigade — often female, Instagram-heavy, talks about systemising your business without specifics)*

What they do in content: High-level frameworks, aspirational lifestyle content, generalised advice about "delegation" and "scaling," occasional mention of AI without any actual implementation knowledge.

Where they fail: They are all promise and no delivery. When they say "automate your business," they mean "hire a VA." When they say "use AI," they mean "try ChatGPT." They lack the technical credibility to actually solve the problem they're raising. Business owners follow them for inspiration but don't buy from them for solutions — because the solutions aren't specific enough to trust.

Where Jess steps in: She delivers the specificity these coaches can't. She names the exact tool, explains exactly what it does, shows the setup, and demonstrates the result. The combination of her strategic framing (business owner's language) and WILBA's technical capability (developer on tap) is something a solo coach cannot replicate.

### Jess's 5 Unfair Advantages

**1. She runs her own AIOS — and documents it publicly.**
Jess's business literally runs on the systems she sells. The audit pipeline, the content generation machine, the automated email responder — all of it is live and operational in her own business. This is not a demo environment. When she talks about AI automation, she's describing something she can see working in real time. A competitor would have to rebuild an entire operating business to replicate this credibility.

**2. The free audit as a content-to-conversion bridge.**
The audit delivers a personalised, branded PDF with the prospect's specific revenue leak and 30-day roadmap — all generated automatically in under 60 seconds. No competitor in this space has anything like this. It's simultaneously a lead magnet, a sales tool, a proof of capability, and a content hook ("I'm going to show you exactly what the audit delivers"). The audit is a moat.

**3. Non-technical translation is her actual superpower — not a limitation.**
In a space dominated by technical people, Jess's non-technical background is the thing that makes her valuable to her audience. She cannot explain things in jargon because she doesn't think in jargon. Every time she explains AI in plain English, she does it in a way that actually lands with the business owner who has the same brain as her. A developer trying to do what Jess does would have to work very hard to un-learn their technical defaults. Jess has this advantage by nature.

**4. The Surf Coast identity is irreplicable.**
A 45-minute drive from Melbourne, Jan Juc. The DJI Mini 4 Pro over the break. The Sony ZV-E1 footage of someone working with the ocean 100 metres away. This is not a brand choice — it's Jess's actual life. And it creates a visual and emotional brand signature that is completely impossible to copy without literally moving there. In the sea of home offices and green screen backgrounds, this is a pattern interrupt that will always stand out.

**5. The gender advantage in an overwhelmingly male category.**
Female service business owners — coaches, allied health practitioners, retreat operators, yoga studios, HR consultants — are underserved by every competitor in this space. They don't want the tech bro. They're not always sure they can trust the SaaS company. But they will follow and trust Jess in a way that has almost nothing to do with what she says and everything to do with who she is. This isn't tokenism — it's genuine market gap. It's also not the core positioning, but it's a structural advantage that compounds over time.

## Section 4: Brand Voice & Tone Guide

### 4a. The Voice in 5 Words

**Straight. Warm. Sharp. Grounded. Unhurried.**

- **Straight** — says what it means, doesn't qualify everything to death
- **Warm** — genuinely likes the people it's talking to, never talks down to them
- **Sharp** — clever without trying to be; the wit is dry, not performed
- **Grounded** — concrete and specific; never floats into abstraction
- **Unhurried** — confident enough not to rush; doesn't chase trends or hype

---

### 4b. Voice Reference Points

**The Dirt Company** — A cleaning product that talks like a smart friend, not a brand. Premium outcome, explained simply. Never preachy. The packaging says more in three words than most brands say in a paragraph. Take: the confidence to say less.

**Matthew McConaughey** — Unhurried authority. Doesn't need to prove himself. Comfortable pausing. Tells stories you didn't ask for and somehow they're always the right ones. The laugh comes from somewhere real. Take: the confidence to have a point of view without needing the audience's approval.

**Dolly Parton** — Disarming. Underestimated. Smarter than the room suspected, and utterly comfortable with that. Self-aware without being self-deprecating. Country when it's useful, sharp when it's needed. Take: the warmth that makes hard truths land gently.

**Dave Grohl (in interviews)** — Passionate without being try-hard. Talks about what he loves with genuine excitement, not performance. Funny because he's honest, not because he's trying to be funny. Take: the authenticity that makes technical expertise feel approachable.

### 4c. Voice Dimensions

**Formal ←—[X]————→ Casual**
Position: 30% formal, 70% casual.
In practice: Jess writes like she speaks — not slang, not textspeak, but the way a smart, articulate person talks over coffee. Contractions always ("it's" not "it is", "you're" not "you are"). First person singular throughout. Short sentences that breathe.

**Corporate ←————————[X]→ Personal**
Position: 90% personal.
In practice: No "we are pleased to inform you." No "our solutions." No third-person brand voice. Jess is always the narrator. Even when describing WILBA as a business, she says "what we do" as a person in the business, not as a brand speaking about itself.

**Technical ←—————————[X]→ Plain English**
Position: 95% plain English.
In practice: Every technical term gets a plain-English translation immediately after it appears, or doesn't appear at all. "AI receptionist" is fine — everyone knows what a receptionist is. "NLP-powered conversational interface" is never acceptable. If a sentence requires technical knowledge to parse, rewrite it.

**Reserved ←————[X]——→ Opinionated**
Position: 65% opinionated.
In practice: Jess has views and shares them without hedging. Not "some might argue" — just the view, stated clearly. She's not a provocateur (she doesn't need to be controversial to be interesting), but she doesn't hide behind neutral language either. "Voicemail is a lead killer" is a position. Take it.

**Polished ←——[X]———→ Raw**
Position: 40% polished, 60% raw.
In practice: Content feels considered but not overly produced. A stumble in a reel is fine. A real reaction is better than a scripted one. The writing is edited (no rambling) but not laundered (no corporate smoothing). The goal is "this person clearly thought about what to say AND sounds like a real human saying it."

### 4d. The 15 Writing Rules

**Rule 1: Start with the point.**
Never bury the lead. The first sentence of a caption or the first line of a reel is the point — everything else supports it. Business owners have no patience for wind-up.
- SAY THIS: "Your physio clinic is probably losing $1,800 a month to voicemail. Here's why."
- NOT THAT: "In today's fast-paced business environment, customer expectations around response times have never been higher..."

**Rule 2: One idea per post.**
A single clear idea lands harder than three good ones muddled together. If you have three things to say, make three posts.
- SAY THIS: (A whole reel about just the 3pm problem — when the receptionist leaves and calls go cold)
- NOT THAT: "Today I want to talk about missed calls, follow-up sequences, and why your CRM matters for client retention..."

**Rule 3: Translate every technical term immediately.**
Any term that a physio, plumber, or real estate agent might not know gets a plain-English translation within the same sentence or the next one.
- SAY THIS: "AI receptionist — basically, software that answers your calls 24/7 and books appointments."
- NOT THAT: "Implementing a conversational AI solution in your inbound communications stack..."

**Rule 4: Use numbers whenever possible.**
Specific numbers are infinitely more credible than vague claims. Never say "a lot of calls." Say how many. Never say "significant revenue." Say what the dollar figure is.
- SAY THIS: "She was missing 12 calls a week. At $180 average booking value and 40% conversion, that's $432 a week — $1,900 a month — walking out the door."
- NOT THAT: "She was losing a significant amount of revenue due to missed calls."

**Rule 5: Say "you" more than "business owners."**
Speak directly to the person reading or watching. "You" is a laser. "Business owners" is a shotgun — everyone hears it but no one feels it's for them.
- SAY THIS: "If you're still relying on voicemail for after-hours calls..."
- NOT THAT: "Many business owners find that after-hours call handling poses a significant challenge..."

**Rule 6: Opinions are stated, not signposted.**
Don't announce that you have an opinion. Just have it. "I think," "in my view," and "it could be argued" are verbal filler that weaken what follows.
- SAY THIS: "Voicemail is a lead killer. Full stop."
- NOT THAT: "I personally think that voicemail could potentially be a barrier to lead conversion in some scenarios."

**Rule 7: Earn the right to the CTA.**
Never open with the ask. Deliver value first — a real insight, a useful number, an honest observation — then make the offer. The CTA should feel like a logical next step, not a tacked-on sales pitch.
- SAY THIS: (Full explanation of the revenue leak calculation) → "If you want to know your exact number — not a rough estimate, your actual number — the free audit calculates it for you. Link in bio."
- NOT THAT: "Want to know how AI can help your business? Book a free audit today!"

**Rule 8: Humour is observational, never at anyone's expense.**
Jess's humour comes from noticing the absurdity in familiar situations — not from mocking clients or competitors. It's the humour of recognition, not superiority.
- SAY THIS: "You know that specific feeling of driving home from work, checking your phone, and finding a voicemail that starts with 'Hi, I called earlier...' — and you already know they've booked somewhere else."
- NOT THAT: "Most business owners are still living in 2005 if they think voicemail is an acceptable lead management strategy." (punching down)

**Rule 9: Short sentences, then one long one for rhythm.**
Three to five short punchy sentences. Then one that breathes a little more, that adds texture, that slows the reading down just enough to let the idea land before you move on. Repeat.

**Rule 10: End carousels and videos with action, not summary.**
The last thing they read or hear should be a direction, not a recap. Never end with "so that's why missed calls are a problem." End with "here's what to do about it" or "the audit takes 3 minutes — link in bio."
- SAY THIS: "Your next step: work out what you're losing. The free audit does it in under 3 minutes. Link in bio."
- NOT THAT: "So as you can see, missed calls represent a significant revenue challenge for service businesses."

**Rule 11: The word "journey" is banned. So is "leverage" used as a verb.**
Both are the verbal equivalent of a lanyard at a conference. If you catch yourself typing "on your business journey" or "leverage this strategy," delete it and say what you actually mean.
- SAY THIS: "As you build this" / "use this strategy" / "as your business grows"
- NOT THAT: "on your entrepreneurial journey" / "leverage your competitive advantage"

**Rule 12: Write for the audience you have, not the one you want.**
Don't write for a future audience of 100,000. Write for the physio in Geelong who is reading this at 8am between clients. Make it specific enough that she feels you wrote it for her. That specificity is what makes 100,000 people follow you.

**Rule 13: Contractions. Always.**
"It's" not "it is." "You're" not "you are." "We've" not "we have." Written contractions make you sound like a human, not a press release.

**Rule 14: The "I" test — make sure it stays personal.**
Every few paragraphs, check: is the brand voice still present as a person? If the writing starts to feel like it came from a company, add one "I" sentence — a personal observation, a story detail, an honest admission — to pull it back.

**Rule 15: Never apologise for having a perspective.**
Jess has opinions about what works and what doesn't in AI automation. She's allowed to share them without hedging. Hedging is insecurity cosplaying as balance. Don't do it.
- SAY THIS: "Call answering services are almost always a waste of money. Here's why."
- NOT THAT: "While call answering services work for some businesses, there may be more effective alternatives worth considering depending on your specific circumstances."

### 4e. Tone Variations by Context

**Educational content (explaining how AI works)**
Tone: Patient but not slow. Like a smart friend who happens to know this really well and wants you to understand it too — not a teacher lecturing a class. Uses analogies from everyday life to make abstract concepts immediate. Slightly lighter and warmer than other content types. Ends with "does that make sense?" energy without literally saying it.

**Revenue/ROI content (the numbers and the money)**
Tone: Precise and a little bit blunt. Like a trusted accountant showing you where your money is going. No softening. The numbers are what they are. The emotional impact is in the specificity, not the drama. Matter-of-fact, not alarming — but clear enough that the reader feels the weight of it.

**Personal/lifestyle content (Jess's world)**
Tone: Relaxed, candid, and self-aware. The least produced-feeling content, because that's the point. Observations over announcements. Story over statement. Slightly more self-deprecating (appropriate) — Jess can laugh at the chaos of building a business. This is where the Matthew McConaughey energy lives most naturally.

**Client results/social proof content**
Tone: Grounded and specific. The results speak — Jess's job is to let them, not perform excitement around them. "Here are the numbers. Here's what changed. Here's what it means for you." Warm toward the client being featured, matter-of-fact about the results, generous with attribution.

**Direct sales content (audit CTA)**
Tone: Direct and warm simultaneously — which is harder than it sounds. Clear about what the audit is, what it delivers, and what happens next. No pressure, no scarcity tactics, no urgency manufactured by countdown clocks. The confidence is in the clarity of the offer itself: "this is what you get, this is how long it takes, this is why it's free." That confidence is more persuasive than any artificial urgency.

## Section 5: Visual Aesthetic Guidelines

### 5a. The Aesthetic in 3 Words

**Coastal. Sharp. Real.**

- **Coastal** — natural light, water textures, the outside world present; not trapped in a studio
- **Sharp** — clean compositions, intentional framing, nothing sloppy or accidental in what's in frame
- **Real** — a real person, a real location, a real life — not a set, not a mood board, not performed

When making a visual decision, ask: does this feel coastal, sharp, and real? If not, it doesn't belong.

---

### 5b. The Mood Board Description

**The environment:** The content world is split between two settings — the coast (beach, garden, deck, outdoor space with natural elements) and a clean interior workspace (minimal, bright, deliberately uncluttered). Both feel lived-in, not staged. The coast content has movement in it — light off water, wind-ruffled pages, the ambient suggestion of salt air. The workspace content is calm and considered — a laptop, a good coffee, light from a window. Never a ring light in a dark room. Never a green screen. Never a corporate conference room.

**Lighting quality:** Natural light only, or natural-light-adjacent (warm practical lighting, soft diffuse windows). No overhead fluorescents. No harsh artificial key lights. Morning light or afternoon light — the golden-blue hours of the Surf Coast. On overcast days, the light is soft and diffuse — still beautiful, just cooler in temperature. This is not a studio operation.

**Colour temperature:** Warm-to-neutral. Not the hyper-warm filters of Instagram lifestyle content circa 2019 — those look dated. Not the cold, desaturated tones of corporate tech content. Somewhere in between: natural, honest, slightly warm, always flattering to skin tones.

**Colour palette:**
- Primary: Deep navy / slate blue `#2C3E6B` — authority, trust, depth. Used in headers, primary brand elements, text on light backgrounds.
- Secondary: Warm sand / off-white `#F5F0E8` — warmth, approachability, the Surf Coast. Used as background, breathing space, contrast to navy.
- Accent: Ocean blue / cerulean `#4A90D9` — energy, clarity, motion. Used for CTAs, highlights, link elements, data points that need to stand out.
- Supporting: Soft slate `#7B8FA1` — for secondary text, supporting elements, the middle ground between the navy authority and the sand warmth.
- Never: Neon colours, aggressive red, hot pink, or anything that reads as tech startup circa 2018.

**Background aesthetic:** In workspace shots, backgrounds are simple — a wall with one considered element (plant, shelf, simple art). Nothing busy. Nothing distracting. The subject — Jess — is always the point. In outdoor shots, the natural environment provides the background. The depth of field (Sigma 50mm at f/1.4) naturally separates subject from background beautifully — use this consistently.

**"Messiness" vs "cleanliness":** This brand has a very specific relationship with mess. The content world should feel curated but not sterile. A coffee cup is fine. Paper on the desk is fine. A pile of random props is not. The Sigma 50mm's shallow depth of field is the cheat code — it blurs background imperfections into beautiful bokeh. Use it constantly for talking head content.

### 5c. On-Camera Presence

**Wardrobe direction:**
- Core palette: Navy, white, cream, sand, soft grey — all clean and calm. The Surf Coast colour palette in clothing.
- Texture is welcome: linen, cotton, knit — materials that breathe and look real, not corporate.
- Silhouette: Relaxed but intentional. Not "just rolled out of bed" but not stiff either. Think: smart casual that photographs well.
- Avoid: Loud patterns (distracting on camera), corporate blazers (wrong brand register), anything that screams "I'm doing a professional video today" — the best content looks like Jess happened to talk to camera in the middle of her normal life.
- Colour note: Avoid wearing navy against a navy background — nothing disappears. Wear white or cream when the background is dark; wear deeper tones when the background is bright or light.

**Hair and presentation:**
Natural. Real. Not the heavy makeup and perfectly set hair of a lifestyle influencer — this is a person who works, lives, and makes content as part of the same life. Hair down and real, or casually up for warmer days. Skincare-forward, not makeup-heavy. The goal is "clearly takes care of herself" not "clearly prepared extensively for this video."

**Background and environment:**
Three options, in order of preference:
1. Outside on the deck or in the garden — natural light, coastal elements, depth of field doing the heavy lifting
2. At the desk / workspace — clean, bright, one or two considered props (laptop, coffee, a green element)
3. Walking and talking (b-roll voice-over style) — adds dynamism and breaks the static talking head format

**Eye line and framing:**
- Look directly at the lens — not at the screen. Get used to where the lens is. Direct eye contact is non-negotiable for this brand's warmth to read.
- Frame: Chest-up for talking head content; slightly looser (waist-up) for lifestyle content. Leave breathing room above the head — don't crop too tight.
- Lens: The Sigma 50mm for talking head (beautiful compression, natural face shape). The Sony 16-55mm kit range for b-roll and location shots.

**Energy and presence:**
- The target energy is "smart friend telling you something important at a café" — leaned in, present, specific
- Avoid: The breathless excitement of influencer culture (fake), the wooden delivery of corporate explainer videos (boring), the performed gravitas of a TED speaker (try-hard)
- Pauses are fine. Real thinking on camera is compelling. If you need a second — take it. That unhurried quality is the brand.
- Smile when it's real. Don't smile because you think you should.

### 5d. Text and Typography Style

**Font personality:**
Two font styles, used consistently:
- **Headlines/emphasis:** A clean, confident sans-serif — geometric, modern, legible at any size. Think Inter, DM Sans, or Neue Haas Grotesk in feel. Bold weight for impact, medium for secondary text. Capital letters only for short, punchy impact phrases — never for body text.
- **Body/supporting text:** A warm sans-serif or humanist sans — slightly softer than the headline font. Readable at small sizes. Canva options: DM Sans, Plus Jakarta Sans, or Outfit.

**Text on video (Reels overlays):**
- White text on naturally dark areas of frame, or dark text with a subtle light backing panel
- Large, legible, not decorating the video — reinforcing the point
- One thought per text card. Never a paragraph on screen.
- Left-aligned or centred depending on the composition — never random placement
- No heavy drop shadows, no gradient text, no rainbow colours

**Caption formatting conventions:**
- Line breaks are your friend. One idea per line, then a break. This creates white space and makes mobile reading comfortable.
- No full stops at the end of lines mid-caption — just break. Full stop only at the end of a complete thought.
- Bold not available in Instagram captions — use CAPS for single words that need emphasis (used sparingly — once or twice per caption maximum)
- Hashtags at the very end, after a line break, ideally separated by "•" from the main caption
- Emoji: used occasionally and specifically — not as decoration but as punctuation or tone signal. Max 2–3 per caption. Never as a replacement for words.

**What to never do with text:**
- No WordArt-style effects — ever
- No more than 2 font weights in any single graphic
- No text so small it requires zooming on mobile
- Never use red for emphasis (too alarming for this brand register)
- No white text on light backgrounds or dark text on dark backgrounds

### 5f. Photography and Video Direction

**B-roll and lifestyle shots that work for this brand:**
- Hands on laptop with ocean or garden in soft background focus
- Walking along the coast (from behind, from the side — not selfie style)
- The Sony ZV-E1 capturing the real workspace
- Coffee being made or held — the ritual, not the prop
- DJI Mini 4 Pro aerial shots of the Surf Coast coastline — used sparingly for pattern interrupt
- Phone screen showing the audit dashboard or an email notification — "business happening"
- Golden hour at the beach — used for atmosphere, not as the main visual

**Shot types that don't work:**
- Stock-style shots (even real ones that look stock)
- Forced "candid" moments that are obviously staged
- Anything with a ring light visible in the eyes — dead giveaway of indoors artificial light
- Conference room or office content — wrong environment for this brand
- Anything with branding that competes (visible brand logos on clothing, other business names in shot)

**The Surf Coast as a visual asset:**
Use the coast as context, not as the entire story. A shot of Jess on a cliff in a blazer talking about AI is powerful — it juxtaposes the location with the substance. A shot of waves with AI text overlay is a screensaver, not a brand asset. The coast earns its place when it creates a contrast: the ordinary place where extraordinary work happens.

**What makes a thumbnail feel like Jess's brand:**
- Her face, direct to camera, with a genuine expression (not performed shock or exaggerated excitement)
- A clear, readable title in the brand typography — either a specific dollar figure or a specific pain statement
- The coastal or warm interior environment in the background
- Two colours maximum on any text elements — ocean blue and white, or navy and white
- Clean and considered — not the maximalist thumbnails of the YouTube clickbait category

### 5g. The Anti-Guidelines — What to Never Do Visually

1. No ring light content — the ring light reflection in the eyes is the visual signature of a studio setup, which is the opposite of this brand's authentic, natural aesthetic
2. No green screens — ever
3. No stock footage — if a shot isn't real, don't use it
4. No heavily filtered or edited imagery — no skin smoothing, no dramatic colour grading, no LUT filters that change the season
5. No flat-lay photography — doesn't fit the business-forward nature of the brand; looks like a lifestyle blogger, not an agency founder
6. No collage aesthetics — multiple images crammed into one frame feels chaotic and dated
7. No cartoons, illustrations, or mascot characters — the brand is a real person; avatars undermine that
8. No all-caps body text in carousels — headers only, and sparingly
9. No gradient backgrounds as primary brand aesthetic — one gradient element as accent is fine; gradient as the main design choice is not
10. No purple — not in the brand palette; tends to read as either spiritual or tech startup depending on the shade; neither is right for WILBA
11. Never crop Jess's head in a thumbnail or graphic — full face, or pull further back; the half-face crop is always unflattering
12. No talking head content against a completely white background — too clinical, too corporate headshot
13. No confetti, sparkles, or celebration graphics as brand elements — undermines the serious ROI positioning

### 6f. Tagline Options

**Option 1: "Your business, handled."**
*Rationale:* Clean, confident, specific. "Handled" implies human-ness (not just "automated") and gives the sense of weight being lifted. Works for every audience segment. The period makes it feel decisive. Strong on merch, in a bio, as a sign-off. Potential weakness: could be generic without context.

**Option 2: "Always on. Never annoying."**
*Rationale:* Directly addresses the core fear (AI sounds robotic and annoying) while delivering the core promise (always on = 24/7 lead capture). The contrast creates a small surprise. Witty without trying too hard. Works best for audiences who've had bad experiences with automated systems. Weakness: slightly longer; two ideas, not one.

**Option 3: "Stop losing leads. Start losing the admin."**
*Rationale:* Two payoffs in one. "Losing leads" is the pain. "Losing the admin" is the aspiration — and the wordplay makes it memorable. Speaks directly to the audience's frustration on both sides. Best for the trades and real estate audience where "admin" is a dirty word. Weakness: requires an extra beat of comprehension; may not work as a standalone one-liner without context.

**Option 4: "AI that sounds like you."**
*Rationale:* Directly kills the #1 objection ("it'll sound robotic"). Also implies customisation and personalisation. Compact. Works on thumbnails. Appeals most to the coaches and allied health segment who are most worried about brand damage from robotic-sounding AI. Weakness: Doesn't convey the ROI dimension — more brand than outcomes.

**Option 5: "Plain English. Real results."**
*Rationale:* The two-part promise from the positioning statement, compressed to four words. Works as a sub-headline paired with any of the above. Does the positioning work, not the emotional work. Best used as a descriptor line, not a primary tagline. Weakness: functional rather than memorable.

**Recommendation:** Lead with **"Your business, handled."** as the primary tagline. Use **"Plain English. Real results."** as the supporting descriptor. Test **"Always on. Never annoying."** for ad copy and carousel headings where the objection-handling is specifically valuable.
